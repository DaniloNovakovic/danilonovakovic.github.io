# **Engineering Ludonarrative Sincerity: A Framework for Intimate Narrative Agents and the Art of the Small Moment**

The contemporary landscape of interactive storytelling is undergoing a paradigmatic shift, moving away from the sprawling epics of traditional high-fantasy or science-fiction towards a more localized, deeply personal form of expression known as ludonarrative sincerity. This movement prioritizes the "small moment"—the quiet, often mundane interactions that characterize human (and inter-species) relationships—over grand mechanical mastery or world-saving stakes. For designers tasked with creating narrative agents or "skills" that serve as tributes to lost companions, such as a deceased pet, the challenge lies in translating the nebulous feeling of "funny-sad" intimacy into a structured computational system. This requires a synthesis of magical realism, environmental storytelling, and advanced agent architecture that respects the primary audience while maintaining a delicate tonal balance.

## **Theoretical Foundations: The Schellian Lenses and the Experience of the Mind**

The construction of any narrative agent must begin with a fundamental understanding of how games create meaning. Jesse Schell’s seminal work, *The Art of Game Design*, posits that game design is primarily the art of creating an experience, which exists not within the code or the assets, but entirely within the mind of the player. This distinction is critical for an intimate tribute game; the designer is not building a simulation of a cat, but rather a bridge that allows the player (the primary audience) to access specific memories and emotional states.

### **The Lens of the Player and Modeling Reality**

Schell introduces the "Lens of the Player" to remind designers that the player’s brain processes complex reality by cutting it down into basic models. In a portfolio game centered on a personal loss, these models are not just mechanical shortcuts but emotional ones. The player’s mind creates a mental model of the lost companion based on small, repetitive behaviors. To achieve ludonarrative sincerity, the narrative agent must present these behaviors in a way that aligns with the player's internal model. This involves "listening"—the designer’s most important skill—which requires an empathetic attention to the audience’s needs and the essential experience being emulated.  
When a player enters a game world, they do so willfully, engaging with a closed, formal system that has its own internal value. For a tribute, this internal value is the preservation of a memory. The agent skill must therefore support the "Lens of the Experience," focusing on the "essential experience" of companionship. This is often achieved through "Flow"—finding the balance between the emotional challenge of the narrative and the player's ability to engage with it without falling into the "boredom" of repetitive tasks or the "frustration" of overwhelming grief.

### **The Elemental Tetrad and Theme Integration**

The design must also be viewed through the "Elemental Tetrad," which consists of Mechanics, Story, Aesthetics, and Technology. For an intimate narrative, these four elements must work in absolute harmony to support a central theme. Schell emphasizes that a story is strengthened by a resonant theme—a universal truth, such as "love transcends physical absence"—that touches players deeply.

| Tetrad Element | Role in Ludonarrative Sincerity | Application to "Danilo" Tribute |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Mechanics** | The procedures and rules of play. | Mundane interactions (petting, feeding) that serve as metaphors for care. |
| **Story** | The sequence of events and narrative arc. | The "Small Moment" focus, utilizing micro-fiction and personal archives. |
| **Aesthetics** | The look, sound, and feel of the world. | "Visually noisy" and "funny-sad" environments that mirror a lived-in home. |
| **Technology** | The underlying software and AI agents. | Use of LLM-based narrative skills to simulate a reactive, "sincere" personality. |

The integration of story and mechanics is further explored through the "Lens of Story," where Schell warns against the "Problem of Time Travel"—the tendency for game mechanics like save-loading to undermine the finality of tragic story beats. In a tribute, the agent must navigate this by making the "finality" of the subject's passing a fixed point, while allowing "freedom" in how the player remembers and interacts with the remnants of that life. This is often structured as a "String of Pearls," where the player has moments of agency within a linear, fixed emotional journey.

## **The Narrative of the Mundane: Magical Realism and Emotional Payoffs**

Magical realism in game design involves the insertion of supernatural or heightened elements into an otherwise mundane, grounded reality. This technique is particularly effective for portraying intimacy because it mirrors the way we often project impossible qualities onto those we love. In short-form games like *A Short Hike* or *Florence*, mundane interactions are the primary vehicle for emotional payoff, rather than a distraction from it.

### **Case Study: A Short Hike and the Freedom of the Simple**

*A Short Hike* utilizes simple mechanics—hiking, soaring, and chatting—to deliver a profound sense of personal freedom and accomplishment. The game does not rely on complex combat or high-stakes puzzles; instead, it finds its "sincerity" in the pace of the interaction. The "magical" element—the anthropomorphic characters and the idealized landscape—serves only to heighten the emotional reality of the protagonist's journey to reach a phone signal. For a narrative agent, this suggests that the frequency and "flavor" of small interactions (like a chat with another "hiker") are more important than the mechanical depth of those interactions.

### **Case Study: Florence and Wordless Intimacy**

*Florence* demonstrates how games can evolve storytelling from traditional media by establishing a non-verbal language. It uses simplistic design to engage players in a narrative that feels like reading a personal book or a diary. The "magical realism" in *Florence* is often found in the way metaphors are mechanicalized; for example, when two characters argue, the player must fit speech bubbles together like jagged puzzle pieces. As the relationship improves, the puzzle pieces become smoother and easier to fit. This provides a direct mechanical mapping of an emotional state—a "skill" that a story agent could use to adapt its interface or dialogue complexity based on the player’s current "relationship status" with the subject of the tribute.

### **Case Study: Mutazione and the Community of Growth**

*Mutazione* is often cited as one of the best-written games for its underappreciated focus on meaningful narrative and "mutant" personal stories. The game centers on a "mushroom scene" where the player has selective agency over how a musical performance plays out, primarily involving writing lyrics for a makeshift band. This interaction is profoundly mundane yet deeply social, emphasizing the reclamation of an abandoned cabin and the creation of something new from the ruins of the old. The "magical" mutation of the plants and the world serves as a metaphor for the transformation of the characters themselves.

| Game | Magical Realism Element | Mundane Mechanic | Emotional Payoff |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| *A Short Hike* | Anthropomorphic animals in a "pure" world. | Hiking and soaring. | The peace of a finished journey. |
| *Florence* | Mechanical metaphors for conversation. | Puzzles as speech. | The wordless understanding of a relationship's lifecycle. |
| *Mutazione* | Mutant flora and gardening as healing. | Writing lyrics and tending gardens. | Community bonding and healing from trauma. |
| *Afterlove EP* | Musical haunting and spectral presence. | Guitar playing as a distraction from grief. | Confronting the immediate stakes of loss. |

## **The Funny-Sad Margin: Environmental Storytelling and Melancholy**

The "funny-sad" margin is a tonal space where humor and tragedy coexist, often referred to through the formula of "Tragedy \+ Time." In game design, this is most effectively realized through environmental storytelling—the use of the world itself to tell a story without explicit dialogue.

### **Environmental Storytelling in Life is Strange**

*Life is Strange* provides a masterclass in both the successes and failures of environmental storytelling. Early in the game, the environments can feel like a "stage play"—littered with posters that provide "false substance" rather than authentic life. However, the game excels when it enters more intimate spaces, such as Chloe Price's bedroom. This room is a "whirlwind of junk," where posters are not just there to be read but to establish a "feel" for the character's tastes. The "visual noise" of the environment inspires the player to search, which "liberates" them from social cues and allows for a deeper connection with the character's past.  
For a tribute to a cat, this suggests that the "agent skill" should not just generate dialogue, but should manage the "environment" of the narrative. This includes the "funny-sad" margin notes in a digital journal—notes that are "messy, funny, sad, and unpolished". These notes represent the "small starts" of a family history, like a sentence scribbled in a margin or a recipe on a grease-splattered sheet.

### **The Mechanics of the "Funny-Sad"**

The formula for the "funny-sad" margin can be modeled as a relationship between the intensity of a tragedy and the temporal or psychological distance from it.  
When Time is low, the Tragedy is raw and overwhelming. However, by introducing "Humor" or "Mischief," the designer can reach a state of ludonarrative sincerity even shortly after a loss. This is seen in *Untitled Goose Game*, where "sincerity through mischief" allows for an earnest exploration of a British village through the irreverent actions of a goose. The "bad affects" like irony and irreverence can actually reach a deeper sincerity than traditional environmental discourse, a concept known as "bad environmentalism".

### **Margin Notes as Narrative Artifacts**

In *Life is Strange*, Max Caulfield’s journal acts as a repository for her inner thoughts and a "second family" to replace the one she lost. The journal is full of "margin notes, highlighted text, and stricken-through passages," indicating a mind that is constantly revising its own narrative. These artifacts are crucial for "protecting Danilo as the primary audience," as they provide a layer of private, intimate communication that feels exclusive to the creator and the subject.

| Narrative Artifact | Function in "Funny-Sad" Storytelling | Emotional Impact |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Stricken-through Text** | Shows the character’s hesitation or self-correction. | Conveys regret or the difficulty of finding the right words. |
| **Margin Doodles** | Adds a layer of levity to heavy topics. | Humanizes the grief and provides the "funny" in "funny-sad". |
| **Fragmented Sentences** | Uses "active voice" and fragments to convey immediacy. | Increases punch and impact while reducing "wordiness". |
| **Overlapping Text** | Simulates the "whirlwind" of a messy mind. | Creates a "visually noisy" and authentic emotional environment. |

## **Sincerity Through Mischief: The Untitled Goose Game Model**

The "sincerity through mischief" found in *Untitled Goose Game* is a vital tool for narrative agents. By allowing for "mischief"—the minor, often humorous disruptions of the mundane—the agent can highlight the earnestness of the world it inhabits. In a tribute to a cat, this might manifest as the agent "knocking over" a virtual vase to remind the player of the cat’s real-life antics, thereby grounding the "sadness" of the tribute in the "funny" reality of the cat’s personality.

### **Irreverence and Dark Play**

This form of "dark play" participates in subverting problematic rhetorical strategies of "pure" or "perfect" mourning. It acknowledges that relationships are not just made of "epic" moments of love, but of "bad" affects like annoyance, irony, and ambivalence. A narrative agent that is "too perfect" or "too sad" feels inauthentic; one that is occasionally "annoying" or "mischievous" feels real.

## **Engineering the Narrative Agent: Skills, Pillars, and Memory**

To implement these theories into a functional subagent or "skill," the designer must move from narrative theory to computational linguistics and agent architecture. An "Agent Skill" is a self-contained package of instructions, code, and resources designed for a specific task—in this case, the maintenance of tone and intimacy.

### **Perception-to-Intention-to-Action Architecture**

Modern AI agents, such as those used for "digital companionship," rely on a multi-layer Large Language Model (LLM) architecture. This architecture is particularly useful for simulating life-like relationships that cannot be pre-programmed.

1. **Perception-to-Intention Layer:** This layer translates the game state and user commands into an "intention." For example, if a player pets the virtual cat, the perception is "physical contact," and the intention might be "express contentment while maintaining a hint of mischief".  
2. **Intention-to-Action Layer:** This layer converts the intention into an executable game command or dialogue line. It requires the agent to understand the "range of executable actions" within the game context.  
3. **Self-Correction Layer:** This layer monitors the game state in real-time to ensure the action does not break the ludonarrative flow or the tonal consistency of the agent.

### **Memory and Neural Networks**

The agent's ability to "form long-term memories through experience" is what makes it feel real and authentic. These memories are stored as vector files and continuously evolve through new interactions. The use of "transformer" architectures with "attention mechanisms" allows the agent to make sense of context and dependencies across disparate data sources—such as the cat's "personality" profile and the player's past interactions.

### **The Brand Voice Framework for Narrative Tone**

The "Brand Voice" framework is a highly effective tool for defining an agent's tone spectrum. This framework ensures the voice stays consistent while the tone adapts to the situation.

| Tone Pillar | One End (Formal/Epic) | Other End (Casual/Intimate) | Application for Tribute Agent |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Personality** | Institutional / Heroic | Knowledgeable Colleague / Friend | The agent as a "second family" member. |
| **Formality** | High-brow / Distant | Clear / Jargon-free / Welcoming | Use of simple, "approachable" language. |
| **Emotion** | Direct / Matter-of-fact | Warm / Empathetic | Dial up warmth in "incident" or "memory" scenes. |
| **Humor** | Serious / Earnest | Playful / Witty | Balance with the "funny-sad" margin. |
| **Inclusion** | Generic / Distant | Person-first / Specific | Focus on the specific identity of the lost companion. |

The agent's "Style Rules" should include policies on punctuation (e.g., limited use of exclamation marks), inclusive language, and the avoidance of ableist or culturally insensitive idioms. For a tribute, the agent should also be instructed on "Industry Jargon Management"—in this case, the specific "inside jokes" or "family language" that Danilo would recognize as authentic to the cat's memory.

## **Protecting the Intimate Audience: Designing for Danilo**

When a game is "secretly a tribute," the primary audience is not the general public, but the individual who suffered the loss. This changes the design priorities from "commercial appeal" to "narrative protection."

### **Selective Agency and the "Story Machine"**

The "Story Machine" method involves creating open-ended worlds to which players can ascribe their own meaning. By providing the player with the tools to "write their own lyrics" (as in *Mutazione*) or "search through junk" (as in *Life is Strange*), the designer empowers the primary audience to reconstruct their own version of the subject. This is a form of "constructivist learning," where the player learns to navigate their own grief through personalized, engaging gameplay.

### **The Power of Specific Writing**

Effective narrative design in this context requires "efficient and specific writing." The moments that are the most captivating are those where there is a "focused, specific image" to latch onto. Over-explaining the world or the character can undermine the story; instead, the agent should provide opportunities for the player to "pay attention" and learn at their own pace. This mirrors the way one learns a pet's personality—not through a manual, but through a series of "small moments" and observations.

### **Conclusion: The Future of Commemorative Agents**

The synthesis of Jesse Schell’s experience-focused lenses with modern AI agent architectures and the tonal nuances of magical realism provides a robust framework for creating ludonarrative sincerity. A narrative agent for an interactive portfolio must be more than a chatbot; it must be a "digital companion" capable of memory, perception, and emotional inflection. By mastering the "funny-sad" margin and prioritizing the "small moment," designers can create tributes that are not just memorials, but living documents of intimacy.  
The integration of "mischief" as a form of sincerity, the use of "visually noisy" environments to inspire search and reflection, and the technical implementation of "intention-to-action" layers ensure that the agent remains authentic to its subject while providing a safe space for the player to engage with their history. Ultimately, these systems succeed when they move beyond "epic scale" and find their heart in the "small starts" of a sentence scribbled in a margin—a digital testament to a life that, while no longer present, remains fundamentally connected to the world.
